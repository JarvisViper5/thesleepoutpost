A Lone Astronomer Tending the Last Observatory on a Distant

A sleep story for adults · Designed to help you fall asleep · ~45 minutes

Read the Opening

Far beyond the familiar constellations, far beyond the reach of any radio signal or satellite transmission, there is a small rocky world circling a quiet orange sun. The astronomers who catalogued it long ago gave it the name Kepler 442b, a modest designation for a place of extraordinary stillness. It is a world slightly older than Earth, its mountains worn smooth by a billion years of gentle wind, its vast plains of copper coloured stone glowing faintly in the amber light of its star.

And on the edge of one of those ancient plains, perched where the land meets the beginning of a long, shallow valley, there stands the last observatory.

You are arriving there now. Let your body settle where it is. Let the weight of your limbs press softly downward. There is nothing required of you tonight, nothing to solve, nothing to plan, nothing to remember. You are simply travelling, slowly, toward a place of deep and quiet purpose.

The observatory is not grand. It is a low dome of pale stone and tempered glass, built to withstand the slow seasons of this distant world. Its walls are the colour of old ivory, weathered by centuries of thin air and starlight. A single light glows amber within, the warm lamp of someone who has been here a very long time, someone who keeps watch so that the stars are not forgotten.

His name is Elias. He is the last of the astronomers assigned to this outpost, a man of quiet habits and patient eyes. He does not rush. He does not need to. Out here, on the edge of an exoplanet two thousand light years from Earth, time moves differently. The nights are long, longer than any you have known, and the sky above is so vast, so dense with ancient light, that to look up is to fall gently into something infinite.

You walk toward the observatory now. The ground beneath you is soft with a thin layer of reddish dust, the kind that rises in little eddies when the wind finds it. The air is cool and carries the faint mineral scent of warm stone slowly releasing its heat. Above you, the stars are already beginning to appear as the orange sun dips below the western ridge, more stars than you have ever seen, crowding the deepening sky like grains of luminous sand.
